# Theoretical Justification: Psychological Flexibility in the Scoping Review

## Why Psychological Flexibility?

At first glance, adding psychological flexibility to a scoping review of structural brain imaging in schizophrenia smokers may seem arbitrary. However, this extension is motivated by converging evidence suggesting that psychological flexibility may represent an unexplored mechanism linking brain structure to smoking behavior in schizophrenia.

## The Behavioral Foundation

Schizophrenia patients who smoke represent a clinical puzzle: despite knowing the health risks, they continue smoking at rates 2-3 times higher than the general population. Traditional explanations focus on nicotine's effects on cognition or symptom self-medication, but these accounts are incomplete.

Psychological flexibility—the capacity to stay present, accept difficult experiences, and act according to one's values—offers an alternative lens. Research consistently shows that schizophrenia patients demonstrate significantly lower psychological flexibility compared to healthy controls, as measured by the Acceptance and Action Questionnaire-II (AAQ-II). This inflexibility manifests as difficulty tolerating negative internal states without reacting automatically—precisely the pattern that maintains addictive behavior.

Importantly, Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), which directly targets psychological flexibility, has proven effective for both populations. Meta-analyses demonstrate ACT significantly improves smoking cessation outcomes, while randomized trials show ACT improves symptoms and functioning in schizophrenia. Most strikingly, a recent RCT specifically tested ACT for smoking cessation in schizophrenia patients, confirming the clinical relevance of psychological flexibility at this intersection.

## The Neural Evidence

If psychological flexibility were merely a behavioral construct with no neural basis, the case for including it in a neuroimaging scoping review would be weak. However, emerging functional neuroimaging research demonstrates that psychological flexibility has measurable brain correlates.

A 2025 resting-state fMRI study directly examined psychological flexibility using the AAQ-II, finding that individuals with greater psychological flexibility showed stronger anticorrelation between the default mode network (DMN) and dorsal attention network (DAN)—a connectivity pattern associated with adaptive cognitive functioning. Lower psychological flexibility was associated with altered connectivity involving the putamen and frontoparietal networks.

Additional fMRI studies of ACT interventions reveal treatment-related changes in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), insula, and prefrontal regions. In opioid-addicted patients receiving ACT, researchers observed decreased activation in the middle frontal gyrus, ACC, and insula following treatment.

## The Critical Overlap

Here is where the theoretical motivation becomes compelling: the brain regions implicated in psychological flexibility by fMRI research substantially overlap with the regions showing structural differences between smokers and non-smokers with schizophrenia.

The sMRI literature on smoking in schizophrenia consistently implicates prefrontal cortex (particularly DLPFC), hippocampus, anterior cingulate, and insula. These are precisely the regions where psychological flexibility appears to have functional correlates. This is not coincidental—these regions collectively support emotion regulation, cognitive control, and value-based decision making, processes central to both psychological flexibility and smoking behavior.

This regional convergence suggests a testable hypothesis: structural differences in prefrontal, cingulate, and insular regions may relate to psychological flexibility, which in turn influences smoking behavior in schizophrenia. Put simply, the brain structure differences we observe between smokers and non-smokers with schizophrenia may partially reflect differences in psychological flexibility.

## The Gap

Despite this theoretical foundation, our systematic searches confirm a striking absence: no study has examined psychological flexibility in relation to brain structure in any population. While functional connectivity correlates of psychological flexibility are beginning to emerge (2025), structural correlates remain completely unexplored.

This gap is particularly notable given that:
- Behavioral research linking psychological flexibility to both schizophrenia and smoking is well-established
- ACT interventions targeting psychological flexibility are effective in both populations
- Functional neuroimaging shows psychological flexibility has neural correlates
- The implicated brain regions overlap with those affected by smoking in schizophrenia

## Conclusion

The inclusion of psychological flexibility in this scoping review serves two purposes. First, it systematically confirms that no existing sMRI study has measured psychological flexibility, validating this as a genuine research gap rather than an oversight. Second, it establishes the theoretical rationale for a primary study that would be the first to examine whether brain structural differences in schizophrenia smokers relate to psychological flexibility—a novel mechanism that could inform both our understanding of why schizophrenia patients smoke and how ACT-based interventions might work.

The scoping review thus functions not only as a synthesis of existing evidence but as a systematic justification for extending the research into psychological flexibility, grounded in converging behavioral, clinical, and neuroimaging evidence.
